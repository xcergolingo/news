About: Astrophysics
Insights: A deep-learning search for technosignatures from 820 nearby stars has been conducted. The authors use ML to process radio telescope data at scale and identify anomalous signals that don’t come from human activity. They’re looking for a very specific kind of radio signal pattern, a so-called “technosignature”: a kind of signal that couldn’t originate from astrophysical sources but would be common in radio communications. Such a signal would be likely to come from a technological source.

About: Space
Insights: Out of 115 million signal snippets, the authors of the deep-learning search for technosignatures from 820 nearby stars found 2.9 million potential events, which get filtered by the model down to a selection of 20,000 non-human-origin signal candidates. Finally, the authors visually inspect those signals to end up with 8 anomalous signals of interest. These signals were not re-observed when looking at the targets again, so at least we know they’re not persistent in time. Without more information, we can’t really conclude anything as to how these signals originated.

About: Technology
Insights: The deep-learning search for technosignatures from 820 nearby stars is looking for a very specific kind of radio signal pattern, a so-called “technosignature”: a kind of signal that couldn’t originate from astrophysical sources but would be common in radio communications. Such a signal would be likely to come from a technological source. There are two big challenges here: 1. automatically finding such signals in the data, at scale, and 2. avoiding false positives, i.e. technological signals that originate from human communications (the telescopes are in radio-quiet zones, but interference is always a concern).
